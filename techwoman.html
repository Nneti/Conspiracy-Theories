Annie Easley

Annie's career sparked when she read about twin sisters who were "human computers" at the Aircraft Laboratory in Ohio. NASA Glenn Research Center was in need of people with strong math skills and luck to the company Annie was in search for a job after moving from Birmingham, Alabama. Annie officially started her career in 1995 as a "human computer" doing computations for researchers which involved analyzing problems and doing calculations by hand;her first work was running simulation for the newly planned Plum Brook Reactor Facility.

When she was hired she was one of only four African-American. As technology evolved so did she therefore she became a computer programmer using languages like Fortran and SOAP in order to support NASA'S programs. Her development involved coding used in researching energy-conversion systems, analyzing alternative power technology used for hybrid vehicles and the Centaur upper-stage rocket.

In the 1970s, Annie returned to school and got her degree in mathematics. Hardworking Easley did her coursework from school with a full time job in NASA. Later in her career, she worked as an equal employment opportunity counselor helping supervisors address of gender, race, and age discrimination complaints in the lower class. In a way, she helped out woman have rights in the Center when her and her supervisor wore pantsuits to work>

In addition to her computer achievements, she also was a champion of employee morale, founding member of the Ski Club and was active in the annual children's Christmas play, Center athletics, and Business and Professional Women's association. She retired in 1989, but remained an active participant in the Speaker's Bureau and the Business and Professional Women's Association. Annie Easley unfortunately passed away in June 25, 2011
